About Digital Certificates

Digital Certificates are used to ensure that the public key in which you are encrypting data belongs to the intended recipient and is not a public key of someone falsely claiming to be the recipient. They are used as form of certificate or identification for an entity and they store information such as public keys, information about the algorithms used, owner and subject details, digital signatures, as well as the time period that the certificate is considered valid.

They are issued by Certificate Authorities who in essence are trusted third parties that verify that public key or other information actually belongs to who it says it belongs to. Before a Digital Certificate is issued, the Certificate Authority verifies the entity and then provides the Digital Certificate. Examples of Certificate Authorities include VeriSign, GlobalSign, GoDaddy, Comodo, OpenCA and OpenSSL.

A Digital Certificate is generally issued in the following format:

  • First the entity requiring a Digital Certificate generates their public and private keys.
  • Next the entity requests a Digital Certificate from a Certificate Authority, who in return requests information from the entity to prove that they are genuine.
  • The entity then sends their public keys and the proof of identity to the Certificate Authority. This information is normally encrypted by the entity prior to it being sent.
  • Once it's received by the Certificate Authority they begin to verify the information.
  • If the information and entity are deemed to be genuine a Digital Certificate is created with the correct information.
  • The Digital Certificate is either sent directly back to the entity or distributed publicly for future use.

Digital Certificate's can also be revoked, this happens when the Certificate Authorities release a list of all the certificates that have been revoked which is known as Certificate Revocation List (CRL). The reason for a certificate being revoked could be the private key was compromised or the Certificate Authority found out that the entity used false information to get a Digital Certificate.

They are four types of Digital Certificates are used on the Internet;

  1. Personal Certificates: these certificates are used to provide an identity to individuals and they are mainly used to provide authentication with users interacting with servers or using secure emails.
  2. Sever Certificates: these certificates are used to ensure that the servers that clients are communicating with are genuine. It is basically a way of authenticating servers.
  3. Software Publisher Certificates: are used to identify to clients that the software publisher is working with trusted by Certificate Authorities but it does not ensure that software is safe to run.
  4. Certificate Authority Certificates: there are two different types of these certificates. One being root certificates that are assigned by the same company that applied for the certificate and the other being intermediate certificates that have been assigned by other companies.

To obtain a Digital Certificate for a website using Google Chrome or Mozilla Firefox, follow the steps outlined below:

Go to a site that provides SSL-Based communication.
Look for the Digital Certificate button in the address bar.
Finally click on the button to see the digital certificate.

To obtain a Digital Certificate for a website using Internet Explorer, follow the steps outlined below:

Go to a site that provides SSL-Based communication.
Click on the browsers File Menu.
Next Click Properties and then Certificates.
Finally click on the Details tab to see the digital certificate.
